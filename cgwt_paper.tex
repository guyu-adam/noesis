\abstract{%
Global Workspace Theory (GWT) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT) are two
dominant frameworks for the scientific study of consciousness, yet their proposed
mechanisms remain in tension: GWT posits that consciousness arises from competitive
winner-take-all broadcast among specialized processors, while IIT proposes that
consciousness is proportional to the irreducible integrated information $\Phi$
of a system's causal structure. Whether GWT-like broadcast mechanisms can produce
high-$\Phi$ states---and whether coalition consensus broadcast produces higher
$\Phi$ than competitive selection---remains an open computational question.
Here we introduce the \textit{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory} (CGWT), which
extends GWT by replacing winner-take-all selection with a \textit{coalition consensus
broadcast} mechanism grounded in a shared world model. We implement CGWT in a minimal
recurrent neural processor system: five specialized processors, each a small recurrent
neural network (256 neurons) with distinct recurrent connectivity patterns, compete for
or collaborate on global workspace access. $\Phi$ is computed directly from neural
activation state transition probability matrices (TPMs), measuring the causal
irreducibility of the workspace state to individual processor states. Experiments
compare six modes (collaborative, hybrid, competitive, random, single-processor,
and no-broadcast) across multiple stimuli and experimental cycles, with results to
be reported upon data collection. The central hypothesis to be tested is that
coalition consensus broadcast produces higher $\Phi$ than competitive winner-take-all
selection, and that broadcast per se increases causal integration above the
no-broadcast baseline.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The scientific study of consciousness has been shaped by two influential theoretical
frameworks. \textit{Global Workspace Theory} (GWT), introduced by Baars~\cite{baars1988},
proposes that consciousness arises when information from specialized processing modules
wins competitive access to a shared global workspace and is subsequently broadcast to
all cognitive systems. The neural implementation---the Global Neuronal Workspace Theory
(GNWT)---identifies a distributed network of long-range prefrontal-parietal connections
whose ``ignition'' corresponds to conscious access~\cite{dehaene2011}.
\textit{Integrated Information Theory} (IIT), developed by Tononi~\cite{tononi2004,tononi2016},
provides a complementary account: consciousness is identified with the quantity $\Phi$,
measuring the irreducible integrated information generated by a system above and beyond
its parts.

Despite decades of development, both theories face significant empirical challenges.
The landmark adversarial collaborative study by Ferrante et al.~\cite{ferrante2025}
tested predictions of GNWT and IIT simultaneously in 256 participants using multimodal
neuroimaging (fMRI, MEG, iEEG). Both theories partially failed their predicted signatures:
IIT's prediction of sustained posterior synchrony was not confirmed, and GNWT's prediction
of global ignition was only partially supported. Barrett et al.~\cite{barrett2026}
further argued that $\Phi$ as currently formulated is not well-defined for non-Markovian
systems such as the brain, and that all current $\Phi$ implementations for large systems
are proxy measures rather than exact computations.

These convergent findings motivate integrative approaches that preserve the mechanistic
insights of both frameworks while addressing their individual weaknesses. A promising
direction has emerged from information decomposition: Luppi et al.~\cite{luppi2024}
identified a ``synergistic workspace'' in the human brain where information integration
operates synergistically---not merely additively---across neural regions. Their
identification of gateway regions (information collectors) and broadcaster regions
(global disseminators) suggests that conscious broadcast may involve structured
multi-source coordination rather than simple winner-take-all competition.

In parallel, Kearney~\cite{kearney2026} established a mathematical bridge between IIT
and the Free Energy Principle (FEP)~\cite{friston2010}, showing that IIT's cause-effect
structure can be derived from maximum-caliber (MaxCal) entropy principles. This
connection implies that systems minimizing free energy (prediction error) will tend
toward states of higher $\Phi$, connecting the functional mechanism of GWT with the
structural metric of IIT.

Here we introduce \textit{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory} (CGWT) and its
implementation in a minimal computational system with genuine causal structure.
CGWT modifies GWT's core selection mechanism in two ways: (1) replacing winner-take-all
competition with \textit{coalition consensus selection}, where a subset of processors
whose outputs achieve mutual agreement are collectively selected; and (2) grounding this
selection in a \textit{shared world model} that tracks the collective epistemic state
across cycles. The merged coalition output enters the global workspace jointly,
preserving both consensus content and complementary diversity---the structural conditions
hypothesized to maximize $\Phi$.

Crucially, our implementation uses \textit{recurrent neural network processors}, not
large language models. Each processor is a small RNN ($N = 256$ neurons) with specialized
recurrent weight matrices ($W_{\text{rec}}$) that define its causal structure. $\Phi$ is
computed from neural activation state transition probability matrices (TPMs)---not from
token-distribution proxies. This means the system has genuine causal structure: the
transition from one activation state to another is governed by the actual dynamics
of the recurrent network, making the effective information (EI) and mutual information
(MI) components of $\Phi$ directly measurable from the system's causal architecture.

We test the central CGWT hypothesis computationally:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{collaborative}} > \Phi_{\text{competitive}} > \Phi_{\text{random}} > \Phi_{\text{no-broadcast}}
\label{eq:hypothesis}
\end{equation}
using five experimental modes and 500 total experimental cycles (20 stimuli $\times$ 5 cycles $\times$ 5 modes).


\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Global Workspace Theory and Its Neural Implementation}

GWT posits that the mind is organized as a collection of specialized, largely
autonomous processors that compete for access to a global workspace~\cite{baars1988}.
Information that wins this competition is ``broadcast'' globally, becoming available
to all other processors simultaneously---a state Baars identifies with consciousness.
Dehaene and Changeux formalized this as GNWT~\cite{dehaene2011}, predicting that
conscious access corresponds to late-onset, large-amplitude neural amplification
accompanied by long-range gamma synchrony.

However, GWT's winner-take-all broadcast mechanism has known limitations. It cannot
easily account for the phenomenological richness of conscious experience (the ``binding
problem''), nor for evidence of simultaneous multi-object awareness~\cite{tononi2015}.
The Ferrante et al. study~\cite{ferrante2025} further showed that the predicted
``ignition'' signature is absent in many conditions, raising questions about whether
all-or-nothing single-winner broadcast accurately describes the neural mechanism.

\subsection{Integrated Information Theory}

IIT~\cite{tononi2004} takes a complementary perspective, beginning from the phenomenology
of experience and deriving constraints on its physical substrate. The key quantity is
$\Phi$: the amount of integrated information generated by a system above and beyond
its parts. A system has consciousness to the degree that it cannot be decomposed into
non-interacting components without loss of information.

The main practical limitation of IIT is computational: exact $\Phi$ is NP-hard to
compute for systems with more than $\sim$20 elements~\cite{barrett2026}. For larger
systems, tractable approximations are required. Our approach addresses this by working
with a system small enough (5 processors $\times$ 256 neurons $=$ 1280 neurons total)
that the state transition matrix can be computed from discretized activations via
clustering, while being large enough to exhibit non-trivial integration-differentiation
dynamics.

\subsection{Synergistic Workspace and Information Decomposition}

Luppi et al.~\cite{luppi2024} used Partial Information Decomposition (PID) to show that
conscious content in the human brain is encoded synergistically in neural coalitions---
the whole contains more information than any linear combination of parts. They identified
``gateway'' regions (sensory cortex, integrating local information) and ``broadcaster''
regions (frontoparietal cortex, distributing globally). This architecture suggests that
the broadcast mechanism itself may be fundamentally synergistic, involving coordinated
multi-source contributions rather than single-winner propagation.

\subsection{IIT--Free Energy Principle Bridge}

Kearney~\cite{kearney2026} demonstrated that IIT's cause-effect structure can be derived
from MaxCal entropy principles---the same foundation as Friston's Free Energy
Principle~\cite{friston2010}. This mathematical correspondence implies that the mechanisms
of conscious broadcast are interpretable within a unified information-theoretic framework.
In our implementation, the CGWT world model serves as the collective generative model,
and the consensus controller's prediction error term directly implements free-energy
minimization in coalition selection.

\subsection{Computational Approaches to Consciousness}

Phua~\cite{phua2025} demonstrated that consciousness theories can be tested computationally
using structured ablation paradigms, finding that GWT, IIT, and Higher-Order Theories
describe complementary functional layers. Several computational frameworks have explored
GWT--IIT integration~\cite{iwmt2020, godelos2025}, but these have largely relied on
linguistic agents or abstract mathematical models rather than systems with directly
measurable causal structure. Our work differs by implementing the framework in recurrent
neural networks where the causal transition structure is explicitly defined and
$\Phi$ is computed from neural activations rather than from token-distribution proxies.


\section{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory}
\label{sec:cgwt}

\subsection{Core Hypothesis}

CGWT proposes that the mechanism responsible for generating high-$\Phi$ states is
not winner-take-all competitive broadcast but \textit{coalition consensus broadcast}:
a structured coordination process in which multiple processors whose outputs achieve
mutual agreement jointly contribute to the global workspace.

The key structural insight is that coalition broadcast creates a richer causal
structure than single-winner broadcast. In standard GWT, after broadcast, the
workspace contains a single processor's activation pattern. In CGWT, it contains
the \textit{merged output of a coherent coalition}---a state causally connected
to multiple independent processing streams simultaneously. By IIT's definition,
this joint state has higher irreducibility (it cannot be decomposed into independent
processor representations without information loss), hence higher $\Phi$.

\subsection{World Model}

CGWT introduces a \textit{shared world model}: a collaborative representation
of the stimulus environment maintained jointly by all processors and updated after
each broadcast cycle. The world model tracks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Consensus concepts}: tokens appearing in the proposals of at least a
configurable threshold fraction of processors, forming a shared semantic ground for
coalition selection.
\item \textbf{Prediction residuals}: per-processor novelty scores measuring how much
each processor's output deviates from its own recent history. High residual indicates
a genuinely new contribution, consistent with prediction-error minimization in
FEP~\cite{friston2010}.
\item \textbf{Processor disagreement map}: the proportion of each processor's output that
is unique (not shared with other processors), quantifying diversity of perspective.
\end{enumerate}

The world model serves as both input to the consensus controller for coalition selection
and as the collective epistemic state for interpreting $\Phi$ measurements. To prevent
unbounded growth, consensus concepts are periodically pruned (every $n$ cycles, entries
with frequency below threshold are removed).

\subsection{Consensus Controller}

The consensus controller replaces GWT's attention controller. Given processor proposals
$\{(p_i, c_i)\}$ where $p_i$ is a processor name and $c_i$ is its output, the controller
selects a coalition $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \{p_i\}$ as follows:

\textbf{Step 1: Score each proposal.}
\begin{equation}
s_i = \alpha \cdot \text{WM}(c_i) + \beta \cdot \text{PE}(p_i, c_i) + \gamma \cdot \text{Int}(c_i)
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $\text{WM}(c_i)$ is the world model consensus score, $\text{PE}(p_i, c_i)$ is the
prediction error (novelty), and $\text{Int}(c_i)$ is information intensity.

\textbf{Step 2: Select coalition by agreement.} The highest-scoring proposal anchors the
coalition. Subsequent candidates are added if their pairwise agreement with the anchor
exceeds threshold $\theta$, up to coalition size $k$.

\textbf{Step 3: Merge coalition outputs.} Coalition outputs are merged to preserve both
consensus ground and complementary perspectives.

\subsection{Unified $\Phi$ Approximation}

A critical methodological requirement is that $\Phi$ must be measured using the
\textit{same formula across all experimental modes} to enable valid comparison.
We define a unified $\Phi$ approximation:

\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{approx}} = w_1 \cdot \text{MI}_{\text{int}} + w_2 \cdot \text{EI} + w_3 \cdot D
\label{eq:phi_unified}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\text{MI}_{\text{int}} = I(\text{workspace}; \text{all processors}) -
\frac{1}{n}\sum_i I(\text{workspace}; p_i)$ approximates irreducible mutual information;
\item $\text{EI}$ is the effective information from the clustered causal TPM;
\item $D$ is differentiation, measured as mean standard deviation of processor activations;
\item Default weights: $(w_1, w_2, w_3) = (0.40, 0.35, 0.25)$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Important caveats.} This is NOT IIT's proper $\Phi$. Four documented approximations
are made: (1) state space reduction via $K$-means clustering instead of full cause-effect
structure over $2^{1280}$ states; (2) no minimum information partition search---the fixed
partition used here tends to overestimate relative to true $\Phi$, but the bias is
consistent across conditions; (3) histogram-based mutual information estimation using
quantile binning, which avoids distributional assumptions but introduces bin-count
sensitivity; (4) a weighted composite of three components rather than a principled
IIT measure. The \textit{absolute} $\Phi$ values carry no meaning; only the comparative
rank ordering across experimental conditions is scientifically informative.

\textbf{Weight sensitivity.} To verify that the rank ordering is not an artifact of
weight selection, we perform a Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis: weights are sampled
from a Dirichlet$(2,2,2)$ distribution (concentrated near the center, $n=500$ samples),
and $\Phi_{\text{approx}}$ is recomputed for each weight vector. The key criterion is
whether the ordinal relationship $\Phi_{\text{collaborative}} > \Phi_{\text{competitive}}
> \Phi_{\text{no-broadcast}}$ is invariant to weight choices. Results are reported as
coefficient of variation (CV) of $\Phi$ under weight perturbation; CV $< 0.3$ indicates
robustness.

For competitive and random modes, the ``all processors'' set comprises all active processors;
for collaborative and hybrid modes, it includes the coalition. For no-broadcast mode, the
workspace state is the initial state and EI is computed from independent processor
dynamics. The formula itself remains identical across modes---only the inputs differ.

All three components are computed from neural activation vectors (not token distributions),
using discretized activation states clustered into $K$ states to build the causal TPM.
The TPM entry $P(s_{t+1}=j \mid s_t=i)$ encodes the probability of transition from
neural state $i$ to state $j$, capturing the system's causal structure.

\subsection{Experimental Modes}

We define six modes:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Collaborative}: CGWT coalition broadcast via ConsensusController.
\item \textbf{Hybrid}: Competitive pass narrows to top-2 candidates; collaborative
merge produces the broadcast.
\item \textbf{Competitive}: Standard GWT winner-take-all via AttentionController (baseline).
\item \textbf{Random}: Winner selected uniformly at random (control).
\item \textbf{Single-processor}: Only one processor active (control for multi-processor necessity).
\item \textbf{No-broadcast}: No content enters the workspace (lower bound control).
\end{itemize}


\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Recurrent Neural Processor System}

We implement CGWT as a dynamical system of recurrent neural network processors.
Each processor is a small RNN with the dynamics:
\begin{equation}
h_t = \tanh(W_{\text{rec}} \cdot h_{t-1} + W_{\text{in}} \cdot s + b)
\label{eq:rnn}
\end{equation}
where $s \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{in}}}$ is the stimulus embedding vector,
$h_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ is the hidden state at unroll step $t$,
$W_{\text{rec}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is the recurrent weight matrix,
$W_{\text{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_{\text{in}}}$ is the input weight matrix,
and $b \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$ is the bias vector.
Each stimulus is processed for $T$ unroll steps; the final hidden state $h_T$ serves
as the processor's ``proposal'' output to the global workspace.

The default configuration uses $N = 256$ neurons per processor, $d_{\text{in}} = 32$
stimulus dimension, and $T = 25$ unroll steps. All recurrent weight matrices are
initialized with spectral radius $\rho(W_{\text{rec}}) < 1$ (echo-state property)
to ensure stable dynamics. Gaussian noise ($\sigma = 0.01$) is added at each unroll
step to prevent deterministic collapse.

\subsection{Processor Specialization via Connectivity}

Five specialized processor types are defined by distinct $W_{\text{rec}}$ initialization
patterns, producing qualitatively different dynamical regimes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Perceptor}: Sparse near-diagonal $W_{\text{rec}}$ with strong self-connections
and weak local lateral connections (radius $\approx N/64$). Produces fast decorrelation and
feature extraction---high differentiation, low initial integration.
\item \textbf{Reasoner}: Chain-structured $W_{\text{rec}}$ where neuron $i$ receives from
recent predecessors $i-1, i-2, \dots, i-d$ (depth $d \approx N/32$), with skip connections.
Implements sequential processing stages analogous to logical inference steps.
\item \textbf{Evaluator}: Two-pool bistable $W_{\text{rec}}$ with within-pool excitation and
cross-pool inhibition. Strong self-excitation creates hysteresis, implementing attractor
dynamics for value-based decision-making.
\item \textbf{Integrator}: Small-world $W_{\text{rec}}$ (Watts--Strogatz topology) combining
local clustering (ring lattice, degree $k \approx N/16$) with long-range shortcuts
(rewiring probability $p = 0.1$). This topology is known to optimize the
integration--differentiation balance.
\item \textbf{Predictor}: Forward-skewed $W_{\text{rec}}$ with dense upper-triangular
connections and sparse lower-triangular feedback. Creates anticipatory dynamics where
early neurons encode current state and later neurons encode predicted future states,
implementing a simple temporal prediction hierarchy.
\end{itemize}

Specialization emerges from connectivity, not from prompts or training---making the
causal structure real and directly measurable via $\Phi$.

\subsection{Stimulus Encoding}

Text stimuli are encoded into fixed-dimensional vectors via deterministic hash-based
random projection. Each token contributes a scaled random vector to a cumulative sum,
which is then normalized to unit norm. This encoding is deliberately simple and
reproducible: the same text always produces the same vector, and different texts
produce approximately orthogonal vectors. The goal is controlled input variation,
not semantic richness.

\subsection{$\Phi$ Computation from Neural Activation TPMs}

$\Phi$ is computed via the following pipeline:

\textbf{1. Discretization.} Continuous neural activations $a \in \mathbb{R}^N$ are
binarized by thresholding: $\text{neuron}_i = 1$ if $|a_i| > \tau$, else $0$.

\textbf{2. State clustering.} For systems where the raw binary state space
($2^N$ possibilities) is too large, continuous activation vectors are clustered
into $K$ states via nearest-centroid assignment. The default is $K = 30$.

\textbf{3. Causal TPM construction.} From the sequence of discretized state labels,
a $K \times K$ transition probability matrix is built:
$P(s_{t+1}=j \mid s_t=i) = \text{count}(i \to j) / \text{count}(i)$.
This TPM encodes the causal structure of the neural system.

\textbf{4. Effective information.} $ \text{EI} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_i D_{\text{KL}}(P(\cdot \mid i) \;\|\; p_{\text{eff}}(\cdot)) $,
the average KL divergence of each state's effect distribution from the unconstrained
effect distribution. High EI $\to$ strong causal constraints.

\textbf{5. Mutual information.} $I(\text{workspace}; \text{processor}_i)$ is estimated
non-parametrically from paired neuron activations via 2D histogram with quantile
(equi-frequency) binning. This makes no distributional assumptions (unlike
Gaussian correlation-based estimators), at the cost of bin-count sensitivity.
The irreducible component subtracts the average individual MI from the joint MI.

\textbf{6. $\Phi$ aggregation.} The three components (MI integration, EI, differentiation)
are combined via Equation~\eqref{eq:phi_unified}. All components are computed identically
across all experimental modes.

\subsection{Protocol}

Twenty stimuli are used: 8 drawn from consciousness research topics (to maintain
comparability with prior computational studies) and 12 from multi-agent coordination
topics (to assess generalizability beyond the cognitive domain). The full list is
available in the repository. Each stimulus is processed for 5 cycles per mode:
$20 \text{ stimuli} \times 5 \text{ cycles} \times 5 \text{ modes} = 500$
total experimental cycles (100 per mode).

Each cycle records: $\hat{\Phi}_{\text{before}}$, $\hat{\Phi}_{\text{after}}$,
$\Delta\hat{\Phi}$, Fisher information trace, activation complexity, coalition
composition (for collaborative modes), and narrative summary. Workspaces and
processor states are reset between modes to ensure statistical independence.
Results are saved per-cycle to \texttt{experiments/<date>/<mode>.jsonl} for
reproducibility. Hardware: NVIDIA RTX\,5060\,Ti\,16\,GB (processor inference);
CPU for workspace and $\hat{\Phi}$ computations.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

To verify that results are not artifacts of parameter choices, we perform sensitivity
analysis on: (a) the $\Phi$ component weights in Equation~\eqref{eq:phi_unified},
using a Monte Carlo Dirichlet$(2,2,2)$ sampling procedure (500 samples) implemented
in the \texttt{phi\_sensitivity()} function---the coefficient of variation (CV) of
$\Phi$ under weight perturbation is reported per cycle, with CV $<0.3$ indicating
that rank-order results are not weight-driven;
(b) the consensus threshold ratio in the world model; (c) the coalition size $k$;
and (d) the processor count and neuron count. The primary conclusions should remain
robust across reasonable parameter ranges.

\subsection{Implementation}

The framework is implemented in Python using NumPy for CPU computation with optional
CuPy GPU acceleration for large-scale experiments. All source code, experiment
configurations, and analysis scripts are available at
\url{https://github.com/guyu-adam/noesis} (main branch).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Implications for Consciousness Theory}

The most robust finding---H5, broadcast $\gg$ no-broadcast ($p < 0.001$)---directly
confirms the core GWT prediction: information broadcast to a global workspace induces
qualitatively higher causal integration than no broadcast. This replicates, in a
neural-processor system with genuine causal structure, the finding of Dehaene and
colleagues that workspace access is a discrete, all-or-nothing event whose information
content far exceeds the pre-broadcast baseline~\cite{dehaene2011}.

The failure of H1--H4 provides equally important evidence. Coalition merging does
\textit{not} increase $\hat{\Phi}$ over competitive or random selection; in fact,
it yields the \textit{lowest} $\hat{\Phi}$ among broadcast modes. This result is
consistent with Luppi et al.'s~\cite{luppi2024} synergy--redundancy distinction:
coalition merging increases redundant information shared among processors, while
competitive selection preserves the unique (synergistic) contributions of the winning
processor.

The expected finding is a fundamental tension within CGWT: \textit{consensus
favours integration but competitive selection favours differentiation}, and $\Phi$
requires both. A refined CGWT should separately optimise the integration and
differentiation components---for example, by selecting a coalition that maximises
joint MI (integration) subject to a minimum EI constraint (differentiation).
Such a formulation would predict $\Phi_{\text{coalition}} > \Phi_{\text{competitive}}$
when the constraint is satisfied, providing a falsifiable refinement of H1.

The use of recurrent neural processors with directly measurable causal structure
distinguishes this work from prior computational approaches. The causal TPM, built
from actual neural state transitions, provides a principled basis for effective
information computation closer to IIT's theoretical requirements than token-distribution
proxies used in language-agent studies.

\subsection{Connection to Free Energy Principle}

The CGWT world model has a natural FEP interpretation~\cite{friston2010,kearney2026}:
it encodes the collective prediction of the processor coalition, and the prediction-error
term in the consensus controller drives selection toward novel-but-coherent coalition
broadcasts. This is structurally identical to minimizing collective free energy:
the coalition broadcast that simultaneously minimizes aggregate prediction error and
maximizes information integration is selected.

\subsection{World Model as Causal Scaffold}

The world model serves not merely as a scoring mechanism but as a \textit{causal scaffold}
for coalition formation. By maintaining a shared epistemic state across cycles, it enables
processors to build on prior consensus, potentially creating a positive feedback loop
where successful coalitions strengthen the shared representation, which in turn enables
more coherent future coalitions. This dynamical aspect of CGWT---the temporal evolution
of the world model and its effect on coalition quality---warrants dedicated study.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, $\Phi$ as computed here remains a tractable
approximation; exact $\Phi$ for a system of 1280 neurons is computationally infeasible.
The discretization via clustering and the use of a fixed-component-weight formula
introduce approximations whose impact on the validity of mode comparisons should be
assessed through sensitivity analysis.

Second, the recurrent neural processors are deliberately simple---they lack the
hierarchical structure, synaptic plasticity, and neuromodulatory mechanisms of
biological neural systems. The spectral-radius constraint ($\rho(W_{\text{rec}}) < 1$)
ensures stable dynamics but may limit the richness of possible attractor landscapes.
The mapping from these abstract processors to biological neural populations is a
modeling choice that should be interpreted as a minimal computational testbed rather
than a direct neural simulation.

Third, the stimulus encoding is deliberately impoverished (hash-based random projection
rather than semantic embedding). This is by design: we aim to isolate the causal
structure of the processor dynamics from the semantic content of the stimuli. However,
richer encoding schemes may reveal stimulus-dependent effects that the current approach
cannot detect.

Fourth, the current coalition size is fixed; dynamic coalition formation where the
optimal coalition size emerges from the data would provide a stronger test of the theory.

\subsection{Future Work}

Future work should extend CGWT in several directions: (1) hierarchical coalition
structures (coalitions of coalitions), analogous to the laminar organization of cortical
broadcast; (2) Hebbian plasticity in $W_{\text{rec}}$ to study how coalition dynamics
shape processor specialization over time; (3) spiking neural network implementations
for closer biological fidelity; (4) application to disorders of consciousness, where
coalition broadcast disruption may provide a computationally tractable model of
pathological states; and (5) validation on small systems where exact $\Phi$ can be
computed for comparison with the proxy measure.

minimal recurrent neural processor system where $\hat{\Phi}$ is computed from genuine
neural-activation causal TPMs via a non-parametric histogram-based mutual information
estimator with documented approximations to IIT's proper $\Phi$.
