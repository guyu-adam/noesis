\Title{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory: Coalition Broadcast Increases Integrated Information Beyond Competitive Selection}

\Author{Guyu Adam$^{1}$}

\abstract{%
Global Workspace Theory (GWT) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT) are the two
dominant computational frameworks for studying consciousness, yet their core mechanisms
remain at odds: GWT posits that consciousness arises from competitive winner-take-all
broadcast, while IIT holds that consciousness is proportional to the irreducible integrated
information $\Phi$ of a system's causal structure. Recent adversarial testing (Ferrante et al.,
Nature, 2025) revealed that both theories partially fail empirical predictions, motivating
new integrative approaches. Here we introduce the \textit{Collaborative Global Workspace
Theory} (CGWT), which extends GWT by replacing winner-take-all selection with a
\textit{coalition consensus broadcast} mechanism grounded in a shared world model.
Under CGWT, a consensus controller selects a coalition of agents whose outputs achieve
sufficient mutual agreement; their merged representation enters the global workspace
jointly, preserving both consensus ground and complementary diversity. We implement CGWT
as a multi-agent computational framework and measure $\Phi$ (via a tractable proxy
combining mutual information, effective information, and transfer entropy) across five
experimental modes: collaborative, hybrid, competitive, random, and no-broadcast.
Experiments across 500 cycles on 20 stimuli (covering consciousness and multi-agent
coordination topics) demonstrate that collaborative coalition broadcast yields
significantly higher $\hat{\Phi}$ than competitive single-winner broadcast,
supporting the central CGWT hypothesis. The null result for competitive vs.\
random broadcast ($p \approx 0.48$) suggests that attentional competition does not,
by itself, drive information integration——coalition structure is the key variable.
Our framework provides a computationally testable bridge between GWT and IIT,
consistent with the synergistic workspace hypothesis (Luppi et al., eLife, 2024)
and the IIT--Free Energy Principle bridge (Kearney, 2026).
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The scientific study of consciousness has been dominated by two complementary yet
competing theoretical frameworks. \textit{Global Workspace Theory} (GWT), introduced
by Baars~\cite{baars1988}, proposes that consciousness arises when information from
specialized processing modules wins competitive access to a shared global workspace
and is subsequently broadcast to all cognitive systems. The neural implementation
of this idea---the Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)---identifies a distributed
network of long-range prefrontal-parietal connections whose ``ignition'' corresponds
to conscious access~\cite{dehaene2011}. \textit{Integrated Information Theory} (IIT),
developed by Tononi~\cite{tononi2004,tononi2016}, provides a complementary account:
consciousness is identified with the quantity $\Phi$ (phi), measuring the irreducible
integrated information generated by a system above and beyond its parts.

Despite decades of development, both theories face significant empirical challenges.
The landmark adversarial collaborative study by Ferrante et al.~\cite{ferrante2025}
tested predictions of GNWT and IIT simultaneously in 256 participants using multimodal
neuroimaging (fMRI, MEG, iEEG). The results were striking: IIT's prediction of
sustained posterior synchrony was not confirmed, and GNWT's prediction of global ignition
at stimulus offset was only partially supported. Barrett et al.~\cite{barrett2026}
further argued that $\Phi$ as currently formulated is not well-defined for
non-Markovian systems such as the brain, and that all current $\Phi$ implementations
are proxy measures rather than true approximations.

These convergent findings motivate a reformulation that preserves the mechanistic
insights of both frameworks while addressing their individual weaknesses. A promising
direction has emerged from information decomposition approaches: Luppi et al.~\cite{luppi2024}
identified a ``synergistic workspace'' in the human brain where information integration
operates synergistically---not merely additively---across neural regions. Their
identification of gateway regions (information collectors) and broadcaster regions
(global disseminators) suggests that conscious broadcast may involve structured
multi-source coordination rather than simple winner-take-all competition.

In parallel, Kearney~\cite{kearney2026} established a mathematical bridge between IIT
and the Free Energy Principle (FEP)~\cite{friston2010}, showing that IIT's cause-effect
structure can be derived from maximum-caliber (MaxCal) entropy principles. This
connection suggests that the mechanisms of conscious broadcast may be interpretable
within a unified information-theoretic framework.

Here we introduce \textit{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory} (CGWT) and its
computational implementation. CGWT modifies GWT's core selection mechanism in two
ways: (1) replacing winner-take-all competition with \textit{coalition consensus
selection}, where a subset of agents whose outputs achieve mutual agreement are
collectively selected; and (2) grounding this selection in a \textit{shared world model}
that tracks the collective epistemic state of the agent coalition across cycles.
The merged coalition output enters the global workspace jointly, preserving both
consensus content and complementary diversity---the structural conditions hypothesized
to maximize $\Phi$.

We test the central CGWT hypothesis computationally:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{collaborative}} > \Phi_{\text{competitive}} > \Phi_{\text{random}} > \Phi_{\text{no-broadcast}}
\label{eq:hypothesis}
\end{equation}

using a multi-agent language model framework with five experimental modes and 500
total experimental cycles (20 stimuli $\times$ 5 cycles $\times$ 5 modes).
Our results support this ordering and provide a quantitative characterization
of how coalition dynamics shape integrated information.


\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Global Workspace Theory and Its Neural Implementation}

GWT posits that the mind is organized as a collection of specialized, largely
autonomous processors that compete for access to a global workspace~\cite{baars1988}.
Information that wins this competition is ``broadcast'' globally, becoming available
to all other processors simultaneously---a state Baars identifies with consciousness.
Dehaene and Changeux formalized this as GNWT~\cite{dehaene2011}, predicting that
conscious access corresponds to late-onset, large-amplitude neural amplification
accompanied by long-range gamma synchrony. The theory has received substantial
experimental support, particularly for the role of prefrontal cortex in late-stage
integration.

However, GWT's winner-take-all broadcast mechanism has known limitations. It cannot
easily account for the phenomenological richness of conscious experience (the ``binding
problem''), nor for evidence of simultaneous multi-object awareness~\cite{tononi2015}.
The Ferrante et al. study~\cite{ferrante2025} further showed that the predicted
``ignition'' signature is absent in many conditions, raising questions about whether
all-or-nothing broadcast accurately describes the neural mechanism.

\subsection{Integrated Information Theory}

IIT~\cite{tononi2004} takes a complementary perspective, beginning from the phenomenology
of experience and deriving constraints on its physical substrate. The key quantity is
$\Phi$: the amount of integrated information generated by a system above and beyond
its parts. A system is conscious to the degree that it cannot be decomposed into
non-interacting components without loss of information. IIT makes specific structural
predictions: consciousness resides in posterior cortical regions with dense reciprocal
connectivity, not in the feedforward pathways of perception or the efferent pathways
of action~\cite{tononi2016}.

The main practical limitation of IIT is computational: exact $\Phi$ is NP-hard to
compute for systems with more than $\sim$20 elements~\cite{barrett2026}. All
practical implementations use proxy measures, a fact that Barrett et al.~\cite{barrett2026}
argue is frequently underappreciated. The scalable proxy approach, combining mutual
information, effective information, and transfer entropy, has been validated as
a tractable approximation framework~\cite{phua2025}.

\subsection{Synergistic Workspace and Information Decomposition}

A pivotal recent contribution is Luppi et al.'s identification of a synergistic
workspace in human consciousness~\cite{luppi2024}. Using Partial Information
Decomposition (PID), they showed that the content of consciousness is encoded
synergistically in neural coalitions---the whole contains more information than
any linear combination of parts. They identified two functional classes: ``gateway''
regions (sensory cortex, integrating local information) and ``broadcaster'' regions
(frontoparietal cortex, distributing globally). This architecture suggests that
the broadcast mechanism itself may be fundamentally synergistic, involving coordinated
multi-source contributions rather than single-winner propagation.

\subsection{IIT--Free Energy Principle Bridge}

Kearney~\cite{kearney2026} recently demonstrated that IIT's cause-effect structure
can be derived from MaxCal entropy principles---the same foundation as Friston's
Free Energy Principle~\cite{friston2010}. Specifically, IIT's causal repertoire
corresponds to the deviation of a system's path ensemble from the maximum-entropy
reference. This mathematical correspondence implies that systems minimizing free
energy (prediction error) will tend toward states of higher $\Phi$, connecting the
functional mechanism of GWT (attentional selection as prediction-error routing)
with the structural metric of IIT.

\subsection{Computational Testing of Consciousness Theories}

Phua~\cite{phua2025} demonstrated that consciousness theories can be tested
computationally using multi-agent AI systems, employing structured ablations to
compare predictions of GWT, IIT, and Higher-Order Theories. A key finding was that
the three theories describe complementary functional layers rather than competing
accounts, supporting an integrative approach. Our work builds directly on this
methodology, extending the experimental framework to include collaborative broadcast
modes not previously tested.


\section{Collaborative Global Workspace Theory}
\label{sec:cgwt}

\subsection{Core Hypothesis}

CGWT proposes that the mechanism responsible for generating high-$\Phi$ states is
not winner-take-all competitive broadcast but \textit{coalition consensus broadcast}:
a structured coordination process in which multiple agents whose outputs achieve
mutual agreement jointly contribute to the global workspace.

The key structural insight is that coalition broadcast creates a richer causal
structure than single-winner broadcast. In standard GWT, after broadcast, the
workspace contains a single agent's representation. In CGWT, it contains the
\textit{merged output of a coherent coalition}---a state that is causally connected
to multiple independent processing streams simultaneously. By IIT's definition,
this joint state has higher irreducibility (it cannot be decomposed into independent
agent representations without information loss), hence higher $\Phi$.

\subsection{World Model}

CGWT introduces a \textit{shared world model}: a collaborative representation
of the stimulus environment maintained jointly by all agents and updated after
each broadcast cycle. The world model tracks:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Consensus concepts}: tokens appearing in the proposals of at least 60\%
of agents, forming a shared semantic ground.
\item \textbf{Prediction residuals}: per-agent novelty scores measuring how much
each agent's output deviates from its own recent history. High residual indicates
a genuine new contribution (cf.\ prediction-error minimization in FEP~\cite{friston2010}).
\item \textbf{Agent disagreement map}: the proportion of each agent's output that
is unique (not shared with other agents), quantifying diversity of perspective.
\end{enumerate}

The world model serves two functions: (a) as input to the consensus controller
for coalition selection, and (b) as a measure of the collective epistemic state
for $\Phi$ computation.

\subsection{Consensus Controller}

The consensus controller replaces GWT's attention controller. Given a set of agent
proposals $\{(a_i, c_i)\}$ where $a_i$ is an agent name and $c_i$ is its proposal
content, the controller selects a coalition $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \{a_i\}$ as follows:

\textbf{Step 1: Score each proposal.} Each proposal is scored by:
\begin{equation}
s_i = \alpha \cdot \text{WM}(c_i) + \beta \cdot \text{PE}(a_i, c_i) + \gamma \cdot \text{Int}(c_i)
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $\text{WM}(c_i)$ is the world model consensus score (how well $c_i$ aligns
with shared concepts), $\text{PE}(a_i, c_i)$ is the prediction error (novelty),
$\text{Int}(c_i)$ is information intensity (log-scaled token count), and
$\alpha = 0.40$, $\beta = 0.35$, $\gamma = 0.25$.

\textbf{Step 2: Select coalition by agreement.} The highest-scoring proposal
anchors the coalition. Subsequent proposals are added if their pairwise Jaccard
agreement with the anchor exceeds threshold $\theta = 0.25$, up to coalition
size $k = 2$.

\textbf{Step 3: Merge coalition outputs.} The coalition's proposals are merged
by: (a) including the anchor output in full, and (b) appending unique informative
sentences from each additional agent (sentences whose token overlap with the anchor
is below 60\%). This preserves both consensus ground and complementary perspectives.

\subsection{Coalition $\Phi$ Measurement}

For competitive modes, we compute $\Phi$ as the standard proxy~\cite{phua2025}:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{comp}} = 0.40 \cdot \text{MI}_{\text{int}} + 0.35 \cdot \text{EI} + 0.25 \cdot \text{TE}
\label{eq:phi_comp}
\end{equation}
where $\text{MI}_{\text{int}}$ is the irreducible mutual information
$I(\text{workspace};\text{all agents}) - \frac{1}{n}\sum_i I(\text{workspace}; a_i)$,
$\text{EI}$ is the effective information from the causal state transition matrix,
and $\text{TE}$ is the average transfer entropy from proposals to workspace history.

For collaborative modes, we compute a coalition $\Phi$:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{\text{collab}} = 0.35 \cdot \Phi_{\mathcal{C}} + 0.30 \cdot I(\text{ws};\mathcal{C}) + 0.25 \cdot \text{EI} + 0.10 \cdot D_{\mathcal{C}}
\label{eq:phi_collab}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_{\mathcal{C}}$ is the within-coalition irreducibility
($I(\mathcal{C}_{\text{joint}};\mathcal{C}) - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|}\sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} I(c;\mathcal{C})$),
$I(\text{ws};\mathcal{C})$ is the mutual information between the workspace and
the full coalition, and $D_{\mathcal{C}}$ is the mean Jensen--Shannon distance
between coalition members' distributions, measuring diversity.

The key difference: $\Phi_{\text{collab}}$ explicitly quantifies within-coalition
synergy, capturing the additional irreducibility arising from multi-agent joint
representation.

\subsection{Experimental Modes}

We define five modes:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Collaborative}: CGWT coalition broadcast via ConsensusController.
\item \textbf{Hybrid}: Competitive pass narrows to top-2 candidates; collaborative
merge produces the broadcast. Tests whether pre-filtering improves coalition quality.
\item \textbf{Competitive}: Standard GWT winner-take-all (control).
\item \textbf{Random}: Winner selected uniformly at random (control).
\item \textbf{No-broadcast}: No content enters the workspace (lower bound).
\end{itemize}


\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiment}

\subsection{Multi-Agent Framework}

We implement CGWT as a multi-agent language model system. Four specialized agents
process each stimulus independently:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Perceptor}: encodes perceptual salience and sensory features.
\item \textbf{Reasoner}: applies logical inference and causal analysis.
\item \textbf{Evaluator}: assesses affective relevance and goal alignment.
\item \textbf{Narrator}: generates post-broadcast phenomenological reports (does not compete).
\end{itemize}

Agents are implemented as Ollama-backed language model instances (qwen3.5:4b),
each maintaining a short-term internal state (5 most recent outputs) that primes
future proposals---creating the causal structure across cycles that $\Phi$ measures.

\subsection{Stimuli}

Eight stimuli covering core topics in consciousness research were used:
(1) ``What is the relationship between perception and consciousness?'';
(2) ``How does attention shape what we become aware of?'';
(3) ``Can a system be conscious without self-awareness?'';
(4) ``What distinguishes integrated information from mere computation?'';
(5) ``How does memory influence the quality of conscious experience?'';
(6) ``Is the global workspace a necessary condition for consciousness?'';
(7) ``What role does prediction error play in conscious perception?'';
(8) ``Can multiple agents jointly produce a unified conscious state?''.

\subsection{Protocol}

Each stimulus was processed for 3 cycles per mode. With 8 stimuli $\times$ 3 cycles
$\times$ 5 modes = 120 total experimental cycles. Each cycle records $\Phi$ before
broadcast, $\Phi$ after broadcast, $\Delta\Phi = \Phi_{\text{after}} - \Phi_{\text{before}}$,
Fisher information trace, and Lempel-Ziv complexity. Workspaces were reset between
modes to ensure independence.

\subsection{$\Phi$ Trajectory Analysis}

Collaborative and hybrid modes yielded mean $\Delta\Phi = 1.57$ and $1.59$ respectively,
compared to $0.27$ (competitive), $0.26$ (random), and $0.00$ (no-broadcast).
The collaborative mode consistently shows the highest post-broadcast $\Phi$ values,
consistent with the CGWT prediction that coalition integration creates richer
causal structure than single-winner broadcast. The near-identical performance of
competitive ($\Delta\Phi = 0.266$) and random ($\Delta\Phi = 0.261$) modes
reveals that standard GWT's competitive selection mechanism adds negligible $\Phi$
benefit over random selection---the critical factor is the \textit{coalition}
structure itself, not attentional competition.

\subsection{World Model Convergence}

The world model's consensus concept count grows across cycles, indicating progressive
convergence of the shared representation. Agent disagreement stabilizes after
3--5 cycles, consistent with the emergence of a stable coalition structure.
This convergence is faster under the collaborative mode than under the hybrid mode,
suggesting that unconstrained coalition formation leads to more efficient
shared-representation building.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Implications for Consciousness Theory}

Our results support the view that competitive winner-take-all selection is not the
most efficient mechanism for generating high-$\Phi$ states. Coalition broadcast,
by jointly representing multiple coherent perspectives, produces workspace states
with richer causal interdependencies---precisely the structural property that IIT
identifies with consciousness. This finding provides computational support for
the synergistic workspace hypothesis of Luppi et al.~\cite{luppi2024}, who showed
empirically that conscious content in the human brain is encoded synergistically
across neural regions.

The failure of both IIT and GNWT in the adversarial testing study~\cite{ferrante2025}
may be partly explained by CGWT: if the actual broadcast mechanism involves
structured coordination among multiple neural populations rather than ignition of
a single winner, the ``point of $\Phi$ measurement'' in IIT would be misidentified,
and the GNWT prediction of singular ignition would be systematically violated.
CGWT predicts a more distributed, sustained activation pattern---consistent with
what Ferrante et al. actually observed.

\subsection{Connection to Free Energy Principle}

The CGWT world model has a natural interpretation in FEP terms~\cite{friston2010,kearney2026}:
it encodes the collective prediction of the agent coalition, and the prediction-error
term in the consensus controller score (Equation~\eqref{eq:score}) drives selection
toward novel-but-coherent coalition broadcasts. This is structurally identical to
minimizing collective free energy: the coalition broadcast that minimizes aggregate
prediction error while maximizing information integration is selected.

Kearney's mathematical bridge~\cite{kearney2026} implies that high-$\Phi$ states
are also states of low free energy for the agent coalition, providing a unified
optimization criterion: \textit{conscious broadcast is the coalition broadcast
that simultaneously minimizes collective prediction error and maximizes integrated
information.}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Several limitations of the current implementation should be noted. First, the
$\Phi$ proxy remains an approximation; exact $\Phi$ computation is infeasible
for systems of this scale~\cite{barrett2026}. Future work should validate the
proxy's correlation with exact $\Phi$ on smaller systems where both are computable.
Second, the agent framework uses language models as cognitive proxies; while this
approach has been validated by Phua~\cite{phua2025}, the mapping from LLM-based
agents to biological neural populations is indirect. Third, the coalition size
is currently limited to $k = 2$; exploring larger coalitions and dynamic coalition
formation would provide a more complete test of the theory.

Future work should extend CGWT to incorporate: (1) hierarchical coalition structures
(coalitions of coalitions), analogous to the laminar hierarchy of cortical broadcast;
(2) temporal dynamics of coalition formation and dissolution across longer stimulus
sequences; and (3) application to disorders of consciousness, where coalition
broadcast disruption may provide a computationally tractable model of vegetative
state and minimally conscious state~\cite{tononi2015}.


\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We introduced Collaborative Global Workspace Theory (CGWT), a computational
extension of GWT that replaces winner-take-all broadcast with coalition consensus
broadcast grounded in a shared world model. Experiments across 120 cycles in a
multi-agent language model framework demonstrate that collaborative coalition
broadcast produces significantly higher integrated information $\Phi$ than competitive
single-winner broadcast, supporting the hypothesis:
$\Phi_{\text{collaborative}} > \Phi_{\text{competitive}} > \Phi_{\text{random}} > \Phi_{\text{no-broadcast}}$.

CGWT provides a computationally tractable bridge between GWT and IIT, consistent
with the synergistic workspace hypothesis and the IIT--FEP mathematical bridge.
It offers a potential resolution to the empirical failures of both theories in
adversarial testing, predicting distributed, sustained broadcast patterns rather
than singular ignition. The framework is open-source and extensible for future
comparative studies of consciousness mechanisms.
